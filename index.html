ABOUT

Ken Croken is a senior vice president at TAG Communications in Davenport and serves as adjunct faculty at St. Ambrose University. Until December 31, 2017, Croken was Chief Marketing Officer for Genesis Health System. Other previous executive leadership and professional assignments include: IBM Corporation (DC), Edelman PR Worldwide (NY), Norstan Communications (MN), Save the Children Federation (CT) and the Legal Services Corporation (DC).

Croken is a graduate of St. Joseph’s University in Philadelphia and the New England College of Law in Boston. He moved to Davenport in 2003 with his wife of nearly 40 years, Kathryn McKnight. They have two adult children, Matthew and Anne, who live and work in New York City. Croken chairs the local organizing committee for Red, White + Boom! He is a past chairman of the Davenport Public Library Board and serves on many boards and advisory committees including, the Putnam Museum and Science Center, Downtown Davenport Partnership, Scott County Family Y and the University of Iowa Community Credit Union, and others.

WHAT IS A.C.T.?

Croken outlined his areas of campaign focus:


“In order to ensure a more responsive and more effective county government going forward; we need to “A-C-T.”
Specifically, we must become more accountable to the changing needs of the people of Scott County;
communicate more clearly plans and progress to meet those needs;
and, fully engage people in county operations through greater transparency.

A.

accountability

C.

communication

T.

transparency


With respect to accountability,


Croken asserts that the county must focus on economic development and collaborate with city and town governments to help ensure a viable tax base for the future. “Even with prudent expense management, we cannot rely on a largely aging population to shoulder the escalating cost of essential services and amenities our citizens expect and deserve,” he said. In support, Croken cites U.S. Census Bureau data regarding changing demographics. “In 2016 the number of Scott County residents 65 and over climbed to more than15%, well above the national percentage,” he noted. “The trend is clear.”

And, when considered alongside the region’s negative job growth, Croken said the situation demands immediate action. “Specifically, the county must partner effectively with others to recruit new jobs and workers to the region as well as better support our current employers. In this way we can attract and retain people who share our Scott County commitment to hard work and community. Based on my work history locally and with the Fortune 500, I have the ‘know how’ necessary to help drive economic expansion and a track record to prove it.”

Croken also maintains that the Board of Supervisors must be more accountable to Scott County residents in its representation of county interests at the state capitol in Des Moines. “As the third most populace county in Iowa, we must heighten Scott County influence to ensure fair treatment and local control on such issues as mental healthcare funding, education equity, minimum wage and county contracting,” he said. “As an experienced and effective advocate and attorney, I will fight for the people of Scott County against misguided party politics as well as attitudes that put the interests of some ahead of others.”


As for communication,


Croken noted that much of his professional career has been spent creating and implementing innovative methods to communicate and engage others. “I’ve spoken with hundreds of people across Scott County. So far, I have found very few with any working knowledge of Scott County government operations. Who are the county board members? Who makes the decisions? What is the scale of the county budget? Which services are provided by the county? The first step toward meaningful citizen involvement is access and openness. This is an area where I can contribute a great deal. And, I promise to do so,” Croken said.

“Certainly, more open communication will drive greater transparency,”


But transparency requires more. It requires public meeting schedules convenient to the public as well as unabridged meeting minutes. Transparency demands public meetings be ‘broadcast’ as well as promoted through social media outreach so that people can follow proceedings without regard to distance, timing, weather or transportation,” he said. “And, transparency demands recognition and respect for the appropriate role of the news media in our system of government. But, above all, true transparency requires elected officials to make accessible all public information not otherwise protected as a routine matter of practice. In short, we do so because we want informed residents, not because we are obligated.”
